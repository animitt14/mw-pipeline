There are significant differences between art and these other asset classes. For example, many asset categories are far more liquid than artwork. Whereas exchange traded stocks, commodities and precious metals are priced continuously in real time, art prices upon which data is available (i.e. public auctions) are updated only on limited occasions during select months of the year when public auctions are held. As a result, these asset categories may appear to be more volatile than art and will react more quickly to events and market forces than art prices. Several leading asset categories are commoditized, so demand is concentrated on the asset category as a whole as opposed to art, which is fragmented into separate artist markets. In addition, most other asset categories have been securitized or otherwise packaged in various types of financial products and instruments such that large pools of capital can efficiently flow in and out of them with relatively low friction in terms of time, effort and cost. By contrast, despite Masterworks ongoing efforts to make art investable, on the whole, artwork transactions remain far more bespoke and less efficient in terms of time, effort and cost than most other asset categories.

02 — The Art Market
A $58 BILLION
Global Market

The global art market is composed of auction houses, galleries, advisors, and collectors engaged in the purchase and sale of unique works. Annual sales stood at $57.8 billion by the beginning of 2025, peaking at $68.2 billion in 2022.

Market Momentum

The fourth quarter of 2025 marked a signaled return of higher-quality works to auction, improved pricing power, and more competitive bidding depth. Record levels of third-party guarantees provided a safety net for consignors and a floor for prices.

Fall auctions saw record-breaking results, including the most expensive modern art ever sold at auction — $236 million for Klimt's Portrait of Elizabeth Lederer — and the most expensive work by a female artist at $54 million for a Kahlo.

The China Factor

Mainland Chinese HNW collectors spent a median of $650,000 on art in the first half of 2025 — the highest globally. At Christie's through 2024, 44% of buyers in Asia were millennials or younger, shifting demand toward works with long-term cultural value.

The return of Chinese capital is a central pillar of the market's 2026 outlook, moving from a period of pandemic-induced hibernation to one of active, high-value participation.

As a tangible, globally traded real asset without a currency-specific denomination, art has historically performed well during inflationary and deflationary periods, functioning as a store of wealth across monetary regimes. 15 — Macro Tailwinds
THE CASE FOR
Art in 2026

Goldman Sachs coined the defining investment framework of 2026: the HALO effect — Heavy Assets, Low Obsolescence. Their data shows capital-intensive assets whose value derives from physical things that are costly to replicate have outperformed capital-light companies by roughly 35% since the start of 2025. Blue-chip art is the most compelling example.

The HALO Effect

Goldman's HALO framework identifies assets whose economic value derives from physical things that are costly to replicate and immune to technological disruption. A Basquiat painting is singular and irreplaceable. While IBM lost 13% on an AI blog post, art continues to recover with Sotheby's posting $7 billion in 2025 sales — up 17%.

Alternatives Stressed

Bitcoin ended 2025 down approximately 6–7% at around $87,000–$88,000. Private equity faced its weakest fundraising year since the early pandemic, with capital raised down approximately 30% versus 2023 highs. Gold and silver have already rallied to historic levels. Art represents a less crowded alternative.

Equity Concentration Risk

40% of the S&P 500 is concentrated in just 5 stocks as of 1Q26. Goldman Sachs projects annualized nominal total returns of approximately 3% through 2034. The CAPE ratio has not been this high since the dot-com boom.

Institutional Consensus

BlackRock, Goldman Sachs, Amundi, Northern Trust, and Apollo all agree: alternatives and real assets are becoming core, not optional. Various industry actors have conviction that the classic 60/40 is evolving toward 50/30/20 models incorporating real assets.

Dollar Weakness

The dollar is down approximately 10% since early 2025. Ray Dalio noted that measured in gold, the S&P 500 actually fell approximately 28% in 2025. If his assessment is correct, dollar-denominated portfolios could be facing real return erosion, and globally priced assets gain structural relevance.
